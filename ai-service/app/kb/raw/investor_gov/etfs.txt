ETFs – Exchange-traded funds (SEC investor.gov). An ETF is a type of fund that holds a basket of stocks, bonds, or other assets and trades on an exchange like a stock. Many ETFs track an index, such as the S&P 500, and offer broad diversification at low cost. You can buy and sell ETF shares throughout the trading day at market price. ETFs are popular with beginners and long-term investors because they combine the diversification of a fund with the flexibility of a stock.

Index ETFs. Index ETFs track a market index—for example, the S&P 500 or a total bond market index. They do not try to beat the market; they aim to match the index’s return (minus fees). Because there is no active manager picking securities, fees are typically low. For investors who want to own the market without researching individual stocks, index ETFs are a common choice. Examples include broad U.S. stock ETFs and bond ETFs; the SEC’s investor.gov site explains how to research specific funds.

Costs and taxes. ETFs have expense ratios that are deducted from the fund’s returns. Lower is better. Some brokerages charge commissions to buy or sell ETFs; many now offer commission-free trading for certain ETFs. When you sell an ETF at a profit, you may owe capital gains tax; holding for more than a year can qualify for lower long-term rates. The SEC advises reading the fund’s prospectus and understanding fees and risks before investing.

Using ETFs in a portfolio. A simple portfolio might include one or two stock ETFs (e.g. U.S. and maybe international) and one bond ETF, with the mix depending on your age, goals, and risk tolerance. Younger investors with long time horizons often hold more in stocks; those closer to needing the money may hold more in bonds. investor.gov has tools and articles on asset allocation and building a portfolio.
