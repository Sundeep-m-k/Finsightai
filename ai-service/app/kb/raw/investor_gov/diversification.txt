Diversification (SEC investor.gov). Diversification means spreading your investments across different assets, industries, and geographic regions so that the poor performance of one does not wipe out your portfolio. The SEC emphasizes that diversification cannot guarantee a profit or protect against loss, but it can help reduce the impact of volatility on your overall returns. Do not put all your money in one stock, one sector, or one country.

Asset allocation. Stocks and bonds behave differently: stocks offer higher potential return and higher risk; bonds typically offer more stability and income. Holding both can smooth out returns over time. How much you put in each depends on your goals, time horizon, and risk tolerance. Index funds and ETFs make it easy to get diversified exposure to stocks and bonds with a small number of holdings.

Sector and company risk. Even within stocks, concentration in one sector (e.g. technology) or one company increases risk. A broad market index fund holds many sectors and hundreds or thousands of companies, so you are not dependent on a single story. The SEC warns that investing in what is "hot" or in a few familiar names can leave you exposed to large losses when those areas decline.

Rebalancing. Over time, some investments will grow faster than others, so your actual allocation can drift from your target. Periodically rebalancing—selling some of what has grown and buying more of what has lagged—brings your portfolio back in line. That can also help you "buy low and sell high" in a disciplined way. investor.gov provides guidance on rebalancing and long-term investing.
