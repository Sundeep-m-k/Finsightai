Understanding risk (SEC investor.gov). All investing involves risk. You could lose some or all of the money you invest. The SEC’s investor.gov explains that different investments carry different types of risk: market risk (prices can fall), interest rate risk (bond prices can fall when rates rise), and inflation risk (your money may not keep up with rising costs). Understanding these risks helps you choose investments that fit your goals and time horizon.

Time horizon. How long you can leave your money invested affects how much risk you can take. If you need the money in a few years—for a house down payment or grad school—you generally should not put it in stocks, because the market might be down when you need to sell. If you are investing for retirement decades away, you have time to ride out downturns and may be able to hold more in stocks. The SEC advises matching your investments to when you will need the money.

Risk tolerance. Risk tolerance is how much volatility and potential loss you can accept without panicking or selling at the wrong time. If a 20% drop in your portfolio would keep you up at night, you may want a more conservative mix of stocks and bonds. There is no single right answer; the goal is to choose a portfolio you can stick with through good and bad markets. investor.gov has quizzes and tools to help you think through your risk tolerance.

Avoiding fraud. The SEC also warns investors to be careful of offers that promise high returns with little or no risk. If it sounds too good to be true, it often is. Check that investment professionals and firms are registered; use the SEC’s Investor.gov and BrokerCheck to verify. Do not invest based on pressure, urgency, or tips from strangers. Legitimate investing is a long-term discipline, not a get-rich-quick scheme.
