Getting started with investing (SEC investor.gov). Investing can help you grow your money over time, but it comes with risk. For beginners, diversified investments like index funds and ETFs spread risk across many companies. Index funds track a market index (like the S&P 500) and typically have low fees. Before you invest, make sure you have an emergency fund and are not carrying high-interest debt. Understand your time horizon and risk tolerance. The SEC’s investor.gov site offers free educational content on these topics.

Why invest? Over long periods, stocks and bonds have historically offered returns that can outpace inflation and grow wealth. That does not mean every year is positive; the market can fall and stay down for years. You should only invest money you will not need for at least five years, and preferably longer. If you need the money for tuition, an emergency fund, or short-term goals, keep it in savings or other safe, liquid accounts.

What is diversification? Putting your money in many different investments instead of one or two reduces the impact of any single investment’s poor performance. A single company’s stock can go to zero; a broad index fund holds hundreds or thousands of companies, so your risk is spread. Diversification does not eliminate loss, but it can reduce the volatility of your portfolio. Index funds and ETFs are an easy way to get diversification with one or a few investments.

Fees matter. Every dollar paid in fees is a dollar that is not growing for you. Index funds and ETFs often have much lower fees than actively managed funds. Over decades, the difference can be substantial. The SEC recommends comparing expense ratios and other costs before choosing an investment. You can find this information in a fund’s prospectus or on the SEC’s EDGAR database.
