OpenStax Personal Finance – Credit and credit cards. Credit is the ability to borrow money with a promise to repay later. Credit cards are a common form of revolving credit: you have a limit, you can borrow up to it, and you repay over time. If you do not pay the full balance each month, you pay interest on the remaining balance. Interest rates on credit cards are often high, so carrying a balance can be expensive. College-level personal finance texts advise using credit carefully and paying in full when possible.

Building credit. Your credit history affects whether lenders will give you credit and at what terms. Paying bills on time, keeping balances low relative to your limit (utilization), and having a mix of account types over time can help your score. Students often start with a low-limit card or a secured card (backed by a deposit). Use it for small, planned purchases and pay the full balance each month to build history without paying interest.

Credit utilization. Utilization is the percentage of your total credit limit that you are using. Many sources recommend keeping it under 30%; lower is better for your score. High utilization can hurt your score even if you pay on time. Paying down balances or having a higher limit (without spending more) can lower utilization. OpenStax and similar textbooks stress that credit is a tool: used well, it builds your record and can help with future loans; used poorly, it leads to costly debt.

When to avoid credit. Do not use credit to live beyond your means or to cover everyday spending when you do not have cash. If you are already in credit card debt, stop adding new charges and focus on paying down the balance. If you are struggling, contact your card issuer to ask about payment plans or reduced rates; nonprofit credit counseling is another option. The goal is to use credit intentionally and avoid high-interest debt that undermines your financial progress.
