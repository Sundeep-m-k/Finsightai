OpenStax Personal Finance – Saving. Saving is setting aside money for future use instead of spending it today. For college students, common goals include an emergency fund, a down payment, or simply building a habit of saving. Pay-yourself-first means moving money to savings as soon as you receive income, before you spend on other things. That way savings are a fixed part of your plan, not whatever is left at the end of the month.

Emergency fund. Personal finance textbooks, including OpenStax, recommend building an emergency fund before investing or aggressive debt payoff. A starter fund of $500 to $1,000 can cover many unexpected expenses and reduce the need to use credit cards. Over time, aim for three to six months of essential expenses in a safe, accessible account. The account should be separate from everyday spending so you are less tempted to dip in for non-emergencies.

Where to save. For short-term goals and emergency money, use a savings account or money market account. These are safe and liquid. High-yield savings accounts pay more interest than typical checking accounts. Do not put money you might need soon in the stock market; the value can fall and you might have to sell at a loss. For long-term goals many years away, investing in diversified funds may be appropriate after you have an emergency fund and no high-interest debt.

Automating savings. Setting up automatic transfers from checking to savings makes saving consistent. Choose an amount you can sustain—even a small one—and schedule it for right after payday. When income or expenses change, adjust the transfer. Treat savings like a non-negotiable expense. OpenStax and similar resources emphasize that building the habit early pays off over a lifetime, even when amounts are small at first.
