OpenStax Personal Finance – Budgeting basics. A budget is a plan for your money: income in, expenses out. The 50/30/20 rule suggests allocating 50% to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt payoff. For students, income and expenses can vary month to month; tracking spending for several months gives you a realistic baseline. Start by listing all sources of income and all fixed and variable expenses. Adjust until the numbers match. Review your budget regularly and update it when your situation changes.

Income and expenses. Income includes wages, financial aid (grants, loans you use for living expenses), family support, and any other money you receive. Expenses are everything you spend: fixed expenses (rent, phone, insurance) and variable expenses (food, entertainment, transportation). The goal is for income to equal or exceed expenses, with a portion going to savings. If expenses exceed income, you must either increase income or reduce expenses; otherwise you will go into debt.

Tracking spending. Before you can set a realistic budget, you need to know where your money goes. Keep a spending log for at least two weeks—ideally a month—and categorize each purchase. Many people are surprised by how much they spend on dining out, subscriptions, or small daily purchases. Use this data to set category limits that you can actually follow. OpenStax and other college-level personal finance texts stress that a budget based on guesswork often fails; one based on real behavior is more likely to succeed.

Adjusting the plan. A budget is not set in stone. If you consistently overspend in one category, decide whether to cut there or reduce another. If you get a raise or a new expense (e.g. a car payment), update the numbers. The 50/30/20 split is a guideline; students with high housing costs or loan payments may need a different allocation. The key is to have a plan, track against it, and revise as needed so that savings and debt payoff remain priorities.
