OpenStax Personal Finance – Debt management. Debt is money you have borrowed and must repay, usually with interest. Not all debt is equal: federal student loans often have lower rates and flexible repayment; credit card debt usually has high rates and can grow quickly if you only pay the minimum. Personal finance courses teach that the order of operations matters: build a small emergency fund first, then focus on high-interest debt before investing.

Good debt vs. bad debt. Some educators use the terms "good" and "bad" debt loosely: "good" might mean debt that helps build assets or human capital (e.g. student loans for a degree) at reasonable rates; "bad" might mean high-interest consumer debt that does not create lasting value. In practice, any debt has a cost. The goal is to manage it: pay on time, avoid unnecessary interest, and prioritize paying off the highest-rate debt when you have extra money.

Repayment strategies. The avalanche method: pay minimums on all debts and put any extra toward the one with the highest interest rate. This minimizes total interest paid. The snowball method: pay minimums on all and put extra toward the smallest balance first. That can provide quick wins and motivation. OpenStax and similar texts present both; choose the one that keeps you consistent. The worst outcome is to ignore debt or make only minimum payments on high-rate cards without a plan.

Student loans. Federal student loans offer income-driven repayment, deferment, and forbearance in certain situations. Know your balance, interest rate, and servicer. Use studentaid.gov to explore repayment plans and see how much you would pay each month under different options. If you have private loans, terms are set by the lender; refinancing might lower your rate but is not always the best choice, especially if you give up federal benefits.
