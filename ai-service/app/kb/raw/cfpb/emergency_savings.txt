Emergency savings (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau – CFPB). Life brings unexpected expenses: car repairs, medical bills, or a lost job. Without savings, many people turn to credit cards or loans and end up paying interest for years. The CFPB recommends building an emergency fund so you have cash when you need it. Start with a goal that is realistic for you—$500 or $1,000 can cover many common emergencies and reduce the need to borrow.

How much to save. A common target is three to six months of essential expenses: rent, utilities, food, insurance, and minimum debt payments. For students or people with irregular income, even one month is a strong start. The idea is to have enough to cover a short income gap or a large one-time expense without going into high-interest debt. You can grow the fund over time; the important thing is to start.

Where to keep it. Emergency savings should be safe and accessible. A savings account or money market account at a bank or credit union is appropriate. Do not put this money in the stock market or other investments that can drop in value; when an emergency happens, you may need to withdraw quickly and cannot afford to sell at a loss. Some people keep emergency savings at a different bank from their main account so they are less tempted to spend it on non-emergencies.

Making it a habit. Set up automatic transfers from checking to savings each pay period. Start with an amount you can sustain—$25 or $50—and increase it when you get a raise or cut expenses. Treat the transfer like a bill that must be paid. The CFPB’s tools at consumerfinance.gov can help you set a goal and track progress. Building emergency savings is one of the most important steps toward financial stability.
