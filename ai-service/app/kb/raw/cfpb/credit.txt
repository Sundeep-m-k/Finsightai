Managing credit (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau – CFPB). Credit cards can be useful for building credit and handling emergencies, but they can also lead to expensive debt when not used carefully. The CFPB advises paying your balance in full each month when possible to avoid interest. If you carry a balance, interest charges can grow quickly; the annual percentage rate (APR) on many cards is high, so even a small balance can cost a lot over time. Use credit only for amounts you can repay, and avoid using cards to cover everyday spending when you do not have the cash.

Understanding your credit report and score. Lenders use your credit history to decide whether to offer you credit and at what terms. A higher score can mean lower interest rates and better offers. You can get free copies of your credit report from annualcreditreport.com. Check reports for errors and dispute any inaccuracies. Factors that affect your score include payment history, how much you owe relative to your limits (utilization), length of credit history, and new credit. Paying bills on time and keeping balances low relative to your limits generally helps your score.

Credit card utilization. Utilization is the share of your total credit limit that you are using. Many experts suggest keeping it under 30%, and lower is better. High utilization can hurt your score even if you pay on time. If you have a $1,000 limit and routinely have a $500 balance, that is 50% utilization. Paying down the balance or requesting a higher limit (without spending more) can lower utilization. The CFPB notes that paying in full each month avoids interest and often keeps utilization low.

When to use credit. Credit is a tool, not free money. Use it for planned purchases you can pay off, or for true emergencies when you have no savings. Avoid using credit to fund a lifestyle you cannot afford. If you are already in credit card debt, focus on stopping new charges and paying down the balance. The CFPB offers guides on repayment strategies and dealing with debt collectors at consumerfinance.gov.
