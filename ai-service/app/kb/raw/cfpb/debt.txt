Dealing with debt (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau – CFPB). Debt can feel overwhelming, but there are steps you can take. First, know what you owe: list each debt, the balance, the interest rate, and the minimum payment. Prioritize high-interest debt (like credit cards) because it costs the most over time. Pay at least the minimum on all accounts to avoid late fees and damage to your credit; put any extra money toward the highest-rate debt. The CFPB calls this the "debt avalanche" approach and provides worksheets and tools at consumerfinance.gov.

Student loans. If you have federal student loans, you have options. Income-driven repayment plans cap your monthly payment at a percentage of your income and can make payments more manageable. Some plans offer forgiveness after 20 or 25 years of payments. Use the Department of Education’s loan simulator at studentaid.gov to compare plans. Do not ignore your loans; missing payments can lead to default and serious consequences. If you are struggling, contact your servicer to discuss options before you fall behind.

Credit card debt. If you are carrying a balance, stop adding to it if you can. Pay more than the minimum when possible; even a little extra each month reduces the time and interest you pay. Some people consolidate credit card debt with a lower-rate loan, but that only helps if you do not run up the cards again. The CFPB warns against debt settlement companies that promise to reduce what you owe; many charge high fees and can hurt your credit. Focus on a steady payoff plan and, if needed, nonprofit credit counseling.

Staying on track. Build a small emergency fund so that unexpected expenses do not push you deeper into debt. Track your spending and adjust your budget so that debt payments are a fixed priority. Celebrate small wins—paying off one card or one loan—and keep going. The CFPB’s resources are free and designed to help consumers make informed decisions about debt and credit.
