Building emergency savings (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau – CFPB). Even small amounts saved regularly can help you cover unexpected costs. Start with a goal that feels achievable—for example, $500 or $1,000. Put this money in a separate savings account so you are less tempted to spend it. Many banks offer high-yield savings accounts that pay more interest than a standard account. Automate transfers from checking to savings each pay period so saving happens before you have a chance to spend. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau provides free tools and guides at consumerfinance.gov.

Why save? Unexpected expenses—car repairs, medical bills, lost income—can push people into high-interest debt when they have no cushion. A small emergency fund reduces the need to put those costs on a credit card. The CFPB recommends starting with a target that fits your situation: even one month of essential expenses is a meaningful first step. Once you hit that, you can grow the fund over time toward three to six months of expenses.

Where to save. Keep emergency savings in an account that is safe and accessible. A savings or money market account at a bank or credit union is appropriate. The interest rate matters less than avoiding risk: do not put money you might need soon in the stock market or other investments that can lose value. The CFPB’s website has resources on comparing accounts and understanding fees. Look for accounts with no or low monthly fees and a competitive rate.

Making it automatic. Setting up automatic transfers from checking to savings helps many people save consistently. Choose an amount you can sustain—even $25 or $50 per paycheck—and increase it when you can. Treat the transfer like a fixed expense so that saving is a habit, not an afterthought. If you receive a tax refund or bonus, consider putting part of it into savings to jump-start your fund.
