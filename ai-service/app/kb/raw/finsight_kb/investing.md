# Index funds and Bogle-style investing for beginners

For college students ready to invest, we recommend low-cost, diversified index funds—the approach popularized by John Bogle. Picking individual stocks is risky and time-consuming; index funds (and ETFs like VOO, QQQ, BND) give you broad market exposure with minimal fees. The principle: you almost always get better long-term results by owning the whole market at low cost than by trying to beat it. Start with one broad U.S. stock index fund or a target-date fund keyed to your expected retirement. Only invest money you will not need for at least five years, and only after you have an emergency fund and no high-interest debt. High-interest debt repayment is a guaranteed return equal to the APR, which usually beats expected market returns.

Why index funds? They hold hundreds or thousands of stocks (or bonds) in one ticket, so you get diversification without research. Fees are low because there is no active manager picking stocks; the fund just tracks an index like the S&P 500. Over decades, low fees compound in your favor. Actively managed funds often charge more and, on average, do not beat the market after fees. For beginners, a single total U.S. stock market index fund or S&P 500 index fund is enough. Adding a bond index fund (e.g. BND) or a target-date fund can simplify further: one fund that shifts from stocks to bonds as you approach retirement.

ETFs (exchange-traded funds) are index funds that trade like stocks. VOO tracks the S&P 500; QQQ tracks the Nasdaq-100; BND is a broad bond index. You can buy one share at a time, which helps when you are investing small amounts. Check that you are not paying commission on each trade at your brokerage. Many brokerages offer commission-free trading for their own or partner ETFs. Reinvest dividends automatically so you benefit from compounding.

When to start: only after you have a starter emergency fund and no credit card or other high-interest debt. If you have federal student loans at low rates, some advisors say you can invest and pay minimums; others prefer paying down debt for peace of mind. There is no single right answer, but never invest money you might need for rent, emergencies, or next semester. The market can drop and stay down for years; you need to be able to hold through that without selling.
