# Emergency fund before investing (Baby Steps)

FinSight follows a Baby Steps–style philosophy: build a small emergency fund before aggressive debt payoff or investing. A starter emergency fund of $1,000 (or one month of essential expenses) reduces the need to rely on credit cards or loans when life happens—car repairs, medical bills, lost hours at work. High-yield savings accounts are the right place for this money: safe, liquid, and earning more than a typical checking account. Once this buffer is in place, you can focus on paying down high-interest debt and only then consider investing. Skipping the emergency fund and going straight to investing leaves you at risk of selling investments in a downturn to cover an emergency, which locks in losses.

Why $1,000 first? It is a reachable target that covers many common emergencies and breaks the cycle of putting unexpected costs on a credit card. For students with very low income or high instability, even $500 can help. The goal is to stop adding new high-interest debt when something goes wrong. After you have paid off high-interest debt, you can grow the fund to three to six months of essential expenses. Essential means rent, utilities, food, insurance, and minimum debt payments—not subscriptions or discretionary spending.

Where to keep the fund: a separate high-yield savings account (HYSA) at a bank or credit union. Keep it at a different institution from your main checking if that helps you avoid dipping in for non-emergencies. The interest rate is secondary to safety and access; the point is to have cash when you need it. Do not put your emergency fund in the stock market, crypto, or any investment that can drop in value. When you need the money, you need it immediately and in full.

When to use it: only for true emergencies—unexpected, necessary, and urgent. A broken laptop for school or a medical bill qualifies; a sale on clothes does not. After you use it, rebuild the fund before resuming extra debt payoff or investing. Making this a habit ensures you always have a cushion and never have to sell investments at a bad time or rely on credit for surprises.
