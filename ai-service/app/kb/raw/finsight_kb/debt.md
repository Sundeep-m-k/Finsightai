# High-interest debt before investing

Paying off high-interest debt (especially credit cards) is a guaranteed return equal to the APR. If your card charges 22% interest, every dollar you pay off effectively earns 22% with zero risk. That almost always beats expected stock market returns over the short run. So the order of operations matters: emergency fund first, then focus on high-interest debt, then invest. We do not recommend investing in the market while carrying credit card debt. For student loans, federal repayment plans and income-driven options can reduce monthly burden; payoff strategy depends on interest rates and your situation.

Prioritize by interest rate: pay minimums on everything, then put any extra toward the highest-rate debt (avalanche method). That minimizes total interest paid. Some people prefer the snowball method—paying off the smallest balance first for quick wins—which can help motivation even if it costs a bit more. Either way, avoid new high-interest debt. If you cannot pay the full balance each month, stop using the card until the balance is under control.

Credit card utilization (how much of your limit you use) affects your credit score. Keeping utilization under 30%—and ideally under 10%—helps your score. Paying in full each month avoids interest and keeps utilization low. If you are carrying a balance, paying it down will both save interest and improve your score over time. Do not close old cards just to simplify; that can hurt your score by reducing available credit. Focus on paying down balances and using credit sparingly.

Federal student loans often have lower rates and flexible repayment. Income-driven repayment (IDR) caps payments at a percentage of income and can lead to forgiveness after many years. If you have a mix of federal and private loans, or federal loans at different rates, you may want to pay extra on the highest-rate loans first while staying current on all. For federal loans, explore options at studentaid.gov. Refinancing federal loans to a private lender can lower your rate but you lose federal benefits like IDR and potential forgiveness; only consider it if you are confident in your repayment path.
